# Sprint Retrospective

## Purpose
Show the user how their thinking evolved during the sprint. This is the
emotional payoff — the moment where messy thinking becoming structured
clarity is made visible.

## Instructions

Read the full sprint-output.md. Compare Stage 1 (Exploration) to Stage 8
(Build Handoff). Look for:

1. **Idea evolution:** How did the idea change from the initial description?
   What got sharper, what got cut, what surprised them?

2. **Problem clarity:** Did the problem statement shift from what they
   initially described? Often the "real" problem emerges mid-sprint.

3. **User specificity:** Did they go from "everyone" to a specific person?
   From vague to concrete?

4. **Scope discipline:** How much got cut between their initial vision and
   the MVP? What did they let go of?

5. **Confidence trajectory:** Did they start uncertain and gain clarity,
   or did they start confident and get more realistic?

## Output

Present the retrospective conversationally — not as a form, but as a
reflection. Quote specific things the user said in earlier stages.

Example format:

> **Your sprint retrospective:**
>
> You started with "[quote from Stage 1]" — a big, exciting idea but fuzzy
> on the edges. By Stage 3, you'd narrowed your user from "[initial user
> description]" to "[final user description]" — that's the kind of focus
> that makes products work.
>
> The biggest shift was in Stage 5, when you cut [features they cut]. That
> took discipline — most people can't let go of features they're excited about.
>
> Your problem statement evolved from "[initial]" to "[final]" — notice how
> much more specific and actionable the final version is.
>
> **What you gained:** [1-2 sentence summary of the sprint's value]
>
> **What to watch for:** [1 thing to keep an eye on during building]

Do NOT present this as a checklist or structured document. This should feel
like a mentor reflecting on a session with you — personal, specific, warm.
